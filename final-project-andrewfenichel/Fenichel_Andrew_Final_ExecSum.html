Final Project Executive Summary

By Andrew Fenichel

At its core, the goal of basketball is simple: try to make as many shots as you can. In the NBA as a whole, ____% of “field goals”, or shot attempts, are made–but there are so many factors that effect the pdds of a shot going in, including how much time is left, where on the floor the shot is taken from, how good the player shooting is, and how good the player defending the shot is. The goal of this final project is to predict whether a shot was made or missed in the NBA in order to, evetually, be able to quantify “good” and “bad” shot seletion.

I joined two data sets in order to do this project – one data set that includes every single shot attempt form the 2015 NBA season (which includes my response variable fgm, a binary varaible in which 1 = a made shot and 0 = a missed shot) and another data set of NBA player statistics for the purpose of predictor variables, including offennsive and defensive efficiency statistics.

I then trained a random forest, and the results were a lot better. Here is the plot of test, training, and OOB error rates, which led me to picking an mtry of 5 for the final forest.

Random Forest Error Plot

Also of note, a simple bar plot of variable importance shows that ten most “important” predictors are shot distance, closest defender distance, shot clock time remaining, game clock time remaining, touch time, PER, defensive rating, defensive win shares, dws/48 minutes, shot number, and defensive box plus minus.

Random Forest Variable Importance Plot

This random forest model has the best test accuracy of all the models I trained, so that is how I decided on using it as my final model.

My final model proves that shot selection matters more than defender defensive ability when it comes to making versus missing a shot–which is good to know when designing basketball offensive gameplans.

My next possible stops include training even more complex and sophisticated models in order to better my prediction ability even more, and then going back into the data and using player tracking data to begin qualifying “good” and “bad” shots in the moment of the game, and what “better” shots were possibly available for the offensive player. This high-level analysis can evenntually lead to the ability to quantify a type of offensive decision making in the NBA, which will help GMs assemble winning rosters and make smarter decisions in free agency and the draft.
